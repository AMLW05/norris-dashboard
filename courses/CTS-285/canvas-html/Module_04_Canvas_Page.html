üé≠ Role Focus: Scrum Master

What does a Scrum Master do?

The Scrum Master is the process facilitator and coach for the Scrum team. They don't manage people or dictate solutions‚Äîthey remove obstacles, facilitate meetings, and help the team continuously improve.

Key Responsibilities This Module:

  • Facilitate daily standup meetings (What did I do? What will I do? What's blocking me?)
  • Track sprint progress and maintain burndown chart
  • Identify and remove blockers (or help the team remove them)
  • Facilitate Sprint Review: Demonstrate completed work to stakeholders
  • Facilitate Sprint Retrospective: Reflect on what went well and what to improve
  • Coach team on Scrum practices and Agile principles

Why This Matters: Teams don't magically self-organize. The Scrum Master creates the conditions for the team to succeed by protecting them from distractions and helping them continuously improve.

üí° Andrew's Tips for Success

  • Standups are NOT status reports: They're about coordination. Keep them short (15 min max). Save detailed discussions for after the standup.
  • Burndown charts show trends, not precision: If your burndown isn't a smooth curve, that's normal. Look for overall direction (trending down = good).
  • Blockers have owners: Don't just document "We're blocked on API access." Who's going to resolve it? By when?
  • Sprint reviews are demos, not presentations: Show working software. Click through features. Let stakeholders interact. PowerPoint is not a demo.
  • Retros need psychological safety: People won't be honest if they fear blame. Focus on the process, not individual performance.
  • Action items must be specific: "Communicate better" isn't actionable. "Send daily progress updates in Slack by 10am" is actionable.

üì£ Andrew's Note

The Scrum Master role is deceptively hard. It looks easy from the outside‚Äî"Just run a few meetings, right?"‚Äîbut facilitating well requires emotional intelligence, pattern recognition, and the courage to speak up when the team is drifting off course.

Sprint 1 retrospectives are always rough. You'll discover that your estimates were wildly off, that some stories were way bigger than expected, or that you didn't actually finish what you thought you'd finish. That's not failure‚Äîthat's data. Use it. Your Sprint 2 planning will be much better because of what you learned in Sprint 1.

If your retrospective doesn't surface at least one uncomfortable truth, you're not digging deep enough. The goal isn't to feel good‚Äîit's to get better.
