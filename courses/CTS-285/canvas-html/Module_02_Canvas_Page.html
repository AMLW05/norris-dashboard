üé≠ Role Focus: Product Owner

What does a Product Owner do?

The Product Owner is the voice of the customer on a Scrum team. They're responsible for maximizing the value of the product by managing the backlog, prioritizing features, and ensuring the team understands requirements.

Key Responsibilities This Module:

  • Identify and analyze stakeholders (who has influence and interest?)
  • Conduct interviews to understand user needs and pain points
  • Translate stakeholder needs into user stories
  • Prioritize features based on business value and feasibility
  • Identify risks that could impact project success

Why This Matters: Poor requirements are the #1 cause of project failure. Learning to truly understand what users need‚Äîand what they say they need‚Äîis a career-defining skill.

üî¨ Hands-On Activity: User Story Workshop (90 minutes)

What you'll experience:

In this interactive workshop, you'll practice writing user stories for a real-world scenario (e.g., "Library Book Management System"). You'll work in small groups to identify user roles, write stories, and critique each other's acceptance criteria.

What You'll Practice:

  • Identifying distinct user roles (not just "user")
  • Writing stories that focus on value, not implementation
  • Crafting specific, testable acceptance criteria
  • Giving and receiving feedback on story quality
  • Splitting large stories into smaller, deliverable pieces

Example User Story Structure:

User Story: As a [library patron], I want [to search for books by author], so that [I can quickly find all works by my favorite writer].

Acceptance Criteria:

  • Search returns all books by exact author name match
  • Results display within 2 seconds
  • Results show book title, publication year, and availability status
  • Search is case-insensitive

üí° Andrew's Tips for Success

  • Listen more than you talk: In stakeholder interviews, your job is to understand, not convince. Ask "why?" at least three times.
  • Conflicting requirements are normal: Different stakeholders have different priorities. Document the conflicts‚Äîyou'll need to resolve them in Module 3.
  • User stories aren't requirements docs: They're conversation starters. Keep them short. Details go in acceptance criteria.
  • INVEST in good stories: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable. If a story doesn't meet these criteria, refine it.
  • MoSCoW isn't just priority labels: Justify why something is a "Must Have" vs. "Should Have." That justification will help when scope gets cut.
  • Risk registers are living documents: Don't just write them and forget them. You'll revisit this in Module 6.

üì£ Andrew's Note

This is where things get real. Requirements gathering is hard because people often don't know what they want until they see it. Your stakeholders might say "I need a dashboard" when what they really need is "timely alerts about system failures." Your job as Product Owner is to dig deeper.

Don't skip the interviews. If you're working on a real project, talk to actual people. If you're working on a hypothetical project, role-play the interviews with classmates or family. The practice of asking good questions is more important than perfect documentation.

By the end of this module, you should have a backlog you could actually hand to a development team. That's the standard. If a developer reads your user stories and says "I have no idea what to build," you need to revise them.
