# Module 8 Activity: Capstone Demo Day - Final Presentations

**Duration**: 120 minutes (full class session)
**Format**: Full class presentation event
**Materials Needed**:
- Projector/large screen
- Each team's capstone project (deployed if possible)
- Presentation slides (optional but recommended)
- Process portfolio (Sprint boards, commit history, documentation)
- Peer feedback forms (printed or digital)
- Celebration supplies (optional: certificates, snacks)

**Learning Objectives**:
- Present technical projects to diverse audiences
- Demonstrate working ML systems with live demos
- Articulate technical decisions and trade-offs
- Defend architectural choices under questioning
- Provide constructive peer feedback
- Reflect on semester-long learning journey
- Showcase PM integration in ML projects

---

## Overview

**This is it.** Eight modules of learning. Eight sprints of building. Today, you show what you've built.

Demo Day isn't just about your ML model's accuracy. It's about telling the story: What problem did you solve? Who cares? How does it work? What did you learn? What would you do differently?

You'll present to three audiences today:
1. **Your peers** (fellow students who understand the technical details)
2. **Your instructor** (who will ask probing questions about your decisions)
3. **Yourselves** (reflecting on how much you've grown since Module 1)

**By the end of Demo Day, you'll:**
- Present a 10-minute technical demo with confidence
- Answer challenging questions about your project
- Provide thoughtful feedback to peers
- Recognize your own growth as an ML engineer
- Celebrate what you've accomplished

This is where learning becomes showcasing. Let's make it count.

## Part 2: Team Presentations (90 minutes)

### Presentation Structure (Recommended)

Each team has **10 minutes** for their presentation. Suggested structure:

**1. The Problem (1-2 minutes)**
- What real-world problem are you solving?
- Who is your user/customer?
- Why does this matter?
- Show a concrete example of the pain point

**Example:**
> "College students waste 20+ hours per week scrolling through irrelevant course recommendations. Our AI-powered course recommender analyzes your transcript, interests, and career goals to suggest the perfect courses for your next semester."

**2. The Solution (1 minute)**
- High-level overview of your ML system
- Key features (3-5 bullet points)
- Show a screenshot or architecture diagram

**3. Live Demo (3-4 minutes)**
- **MOST IMPORTANT PART**
- Show your system working with real inputs
- Narrate what's happening: "Now the model is predicting... You can see the confidence score here..."
- Have 2-3 demo scenarios prepared
- If demo breaks, have backup: recorded video or screenshots

**4. Technical Deep Dive (2-3 minutes)**
- What ML algorithms did you use and why?
- How did you handle data preparation/feature engineering?
- Architecture diagram (show layers, data flow)
- Key metrics: accuracy, precision, recall, etc.
- What technical challenges did you face and how did you solve them?

**Example:**
> "We compared 5 classification algorithms. Random Forest won with 87% accuracy. We initially tried neural networks but they overfit on our small dataset. Transfer learning with ResNet50 didn't help because course data isn't visual. So we pivoted to ensemble methods with feature engineering on GPA, major, and historical course patterns."

**5. PM/Process Integration (1-2 minutes)**
- Show your Sprint board evolution (Sprint 1 â†’ Sprint 8)
- Velocity chart: Did you get better at estimating?
- One key retrospective insight: What did you learn about working as a team?
- How did Scrum help or hurt your ML project?

**6. Lessons Learned (1 minute)**
- What would you do differently?
- What are you most proud of?
- One technical lesson learned
- One process lesson learned

**7. Q&A (5 minutes)**
- Audience asks questions
- Instructor may ask probing questions:
  - "Why did you choose X algorithm over Y?"
  - "How would you handle 100x more data?"
  - "What's your model's biggest weakness?"
  - "If you had 2 more weeks, what would you improve?"

## Teaching Philosophy Notes

**Demo Day is validation.** Students have worked hard for 8 weeks. Many doubted themselves. Today they see: "I built this. I understand this. I can do this." That confidence is as important as the technical skills.

**Presentation skills matter.** The best ML engineer who can't explain their work is less valuable than the good ML engineer who can communicate clearly. Use Demo Day to reinforce: communication is a core competency.

**Process counts.** It's tempting to focus only on model accuracy. Resist. Ask about sprint planning, retrospectives, velocity. Show students that the PM skills they learned are professional skills that matter.

**Failure is part of the story.** Encourage teams to talk about what went wrong. "Our model initially got 60% accuracy, here's how we debugged it" is more compelling than "We got 95% on the first try" (which is probably a lie anyway).

**Peer feedback is a gift.** Students often give better, more actionable feedback than instructors because they're in the trenches together. Model how to give constructive feedback, and they'll internalize it.

**Celebrate.** Seriously. These students worked hard. They deserve recognition. Don't skip the celebration part. Print the certificates. Take the photos. Play the music. Make it memorable.
